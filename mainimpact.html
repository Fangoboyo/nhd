Short Term Impact

What changed after a few years after US imposed multiple sanctions?

Congress passes the Iran-Iraq Arms Nonproliferation Act of 1992, which doesn't allow the transfer of controlled goods or technology that might contribute “knowingly and materially” to Iran's proliferation of advanced conventional weapons.

A graph and a pie chart showing it's electricity and oil production in
relation to its energy sectors. Source: ResearchGate
After years of continuous failed diplomatic efforts to negotiate with Iran, European officials concluded that sanctions were necessary to get Iran to agree with its international obligations, including the Nuclear Non Proliferation Treaty. In 1996, the U.S. Congress passed the Iran-Libya Sanctions Act, which penalizes foreign and U.S. investment exceeding $20 million in Iran's energy sector in one year. Since 2007, Europe has been as involved as the United States in diplomatic efforts to secure U.N. sanctions on dual-use items useful for Iran's nuclear and missile programs.
The International Atomic Energy Agency Board of Governors designs a resolution calling for Iran to suspend all enrichment and reprocessing. The resolution requires Iran to declare all material relevant to its uranium-enrichment program and allow the inspectors to conduct environmental sampling at any location. The deadline for this was October 31st, 2003.
